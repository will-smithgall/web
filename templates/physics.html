Additional Physics Courses

One Additional Course From the Provided List is Need

PHYS 0481

This course, a follow up to PHYS 0477 (Principles of Modern Physics 1), will cover topics which include: quantum mechanics of many particle systems, multielectron atoms and the periodic table, basics of quantum stastical mechanics, introduction to solid state physics (conductors, semiconductors, superconductors), basic nuclear physics, and an introduction to elementary particle physics. This course will be an introduction to the concepts and techinques necessary for understanding recent fundamental research and tecnology.

PHYS 1374

This course, Introduction to Solid State Physics, will cover some of the basic ideas of solid state physics. Materials will be taught through textbook, as well as the possibility for a "flipped classroom" through some of the professors

PHYS 1375

This course, Foundations of Nanoscience, will overview issues that arise of physical systems at nanometer length scales. The majority of the course will cover basic physics concepts such as quantam mechanis, electromagnetism and statistical mechanics and how these concepts shift when the lengths and amount of atoms in the system get increasingly small.

PHYS 1376

This course, Introduction to Biological Physics, is designed for students who are interested in studying life phenomena from a physics perspective. Important concepts from this course inclue: different energy forms and their transformations, Newton's law of motion for solid and soft bodies, electrostatics and molecular interactions, physics of reversible and irreversible processes, information processing and transmission, and the law of exponentials.

PHYS 1378

This course, Introduction to Nulcear and Particle Physics, will cover a variety of topics, including: typical experimental techniques for particle detection and acceleration, calculating particle physics processes based on kinematics and conservation laws, the role of symmetries as an oganizing principle, the strucle of the standard model of particle physics, and phenomenological connections between results of different laboratory and astrophysical experiments.
